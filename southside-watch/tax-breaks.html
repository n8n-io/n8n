Types of Tax Incentives Being Offered

1. Payment in Lieu of Taxes (PILOT)

A PILOT agreement lets a company pay a negotiated, reduced amount instead of the normal property tax rate — typically for a fixed period (10–15 years). During that period, the company pays less than its fair share of local taxes, while schools, roads, fire departments, and other public services continue to need funding from remaining taxpayers.

Brunswick Example: Apex Digital's 15-year PILOT agreement means Brunswick County taxpayers subsidize the facility for 15 years before full property tax kicks in. The complete agreement was not released to the public before the 4-1 vote approving it.

2. Enterprise Zone Tax Credits

Virginia's Enterprise Zone program offers state and local tax credits for businesses in designated zones. Data center projects sometimes qualify, layering state subsidies on top of local deals.

3. Infrastructure Grants (EDA)

Economic Development Authorities (EDAs) can issue grants from public funds for infrastructure — roads, water lines, utility extensions — that benefit a specific project. These grants come from public money.

Brunswick Example: The Brunswick County EDA awarded $8 million in infrastructure grants for the Apex Digital project. This is public money — from local taxpayers and state allocations — going to private infrastructure serving a private corporate interest.

4. Property Tax Abatements

Greensville County's proposed deal includes a 10-year property tax abatement — meaning the facility pays little or no property taxes for a decade, during which time the infrastructure it requires must still be maintained by other taxpayers.

5. Expedited Permitting

South Hill/Mecklenburg's deal included expedited permitting — giving the developer faster regulatory processing than other businesses receive. This is a form of public subsidy: staff time and priority access are public resources.

⚠ Critical Transparency Failure: In every Southside Virginia case tracked, residents were not provided full agreement text before their local board voted. This is unacceptable. No public body should vote to give away public money — in any form — without the full agreement being available for public review for a minimum of 30 days prior to the vote.

☑ What to Demand Before Any Vote

  • Release the full PILOT or incentive agreement 30 days before the vote
  • Disclose the total estimated value of all incentives over their full term
  • Require an independent fiscal impact analysis
  • Include binding clawback clauses if job/revenue promises aren't met
  • Require annual public reporting on compliance
  • Prohibit NDAs on agreements involving public funds

🔴 Red Flags to Watch For

  • → Agreement not released before vote
  • → Public hearing with less than 2 weeks notice
  • → NDA on developer or end user identity
  • → No fiscal impact study requested
  • → No clawback provisions
  • → Infrastructure costs left unresolved at vote

Public subsidies can be appropriate tools for economic development — but only when they are transparent, fairly valued, come with enforceable commitments, and represent a genuine community benefit. Signing away decades of public revenue for 35 jobs, with no public review of the agreement, is not economic development. It is a gift wrapped in a press release.
