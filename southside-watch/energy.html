How Data Centers Strain the Power Grid

A single 100 MW data center consumes as much electricity as a small city. When dozens of projects cluster in one region, the transmission and distribution infrastructure must be significantly upgraded — at enormous cost.

⚠ The Key Question: When Dominion Energy or Appalachian Power must build new substations and transmission lines to serve data centers, who pays? Historically in Virginia, grid upgrade costs are often "socialized" — spread across all ratepayers, including residential customers who never asked for the data center.

What "Cost Socialization" Means for You

The Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC) regulates utility rate increases. When utilities build infrastructure for large industrial customers, they can — and do — seek rate recovery that includes all residential customers. If you have a Dominion Energy bill, you could be paying for substations built for data centers you've never heard of.

The Brunswick Example

The Apex Digital facility approved in Brunswick County in January 2025 is expected to require a new or significantly upgraded substation. Dominion Energy filings suggest this could cost $47 million. As of this writing, no clear public statement has been made about whether the developer will bear this cost or whether it will be passed to ratepayers.

⚠ Still Unanswered: Brunswick County's Board of Supervisors approved the data center rezoning without requiring a definitive public answer about who pays for the substation upgrade. Residents should demand this answer before any further approvals.

Backup Diesel Generators

Data centers require uninterrupted power. They typically deploy large diesel backup generators — often dozens of them — that can run for days during grid outages or peak demand periods. In Brunswick County, the approved facility includes 12 diesel generators producing 2 MW each.

These generators:

  • Emit nitrogen oxides, particulate matter, and carbon dioxide
  • Can run for extended periods during testing (often monthly)
  • Create significant noise in rural communities
  • Require diesel fuel storage — creating spill risk
  • May require air quality permits — but enforcement varies

How Virginia Electric Rate Increases Work

1

Utility Builds Infrastructure

Dominion Energy or other utilities build new substations, transmission lines, or distribution upgrades to serve new large customers like data centers.

2

SCC Rate Case Filed

The utility files a rate case with the State Corporation Commission to recover those infrastructure costs — spread across all customers in their service territory.

3

Residents Pay the Bill

If approved, every residential customer — including low-income families — pays a portion of infrastructure costs built to serve large corporations that negotiated special rates.

The question is not whether data centers need power. The question is: who pays when the grid must be expanded to serve them? In many Virginia cases, the answer has been: everyone — including residents who had no say in whether the data center was built.

Virginia law allows utilities to seek rate recovery for infrastructure investments. The degree of impact depends on how costs are allocated, whether the developer bears any direct infrastructure cost, and whether the SCC approves cost socialization.

The most important protection residents can demand is a direct cost allocation agreement — requiring the data center developer to pay for dedicated infrastructure, not spread those costs to the public.

A 100 MW data center running 24/7 consumes approximately 876,000 megawatt-hours per year. That's roughly equivalent to the annual electricity use of 80,000–90,000 average Virginia homes. At scale, a cluster of 500 MW of data centers in a rural region would rival the power consumption of a medium-sized city.

Data centers require uninterrupted power. Large facilities deploy banks of diesel generators — sometimes dozens — capable of running the entire facility if grid power fails. These generators can also be activated during peak grid demand periods.

  • A 100 MW facility might have 20–50 diesel generators
  • Monthly testing can involve running all generators simultaneously for 30+ minutes
  • Diesel exhaust contains particulate matter and nitrogen oxides — health concerns for nearby residents
  • Extended outages (like hurricanes) can mean generators run for days in densely populated rural communities
  • Air quality permits exist but enforcement in rural Virginia has historically been limited

At minimum, responsible local governments should require:

  • A clear, binding commitment on who pays for grid infrastructure upgrades
  • Independent assessment of impact on residential electric rates
  • Air quality permits for backup diesel generators, with enforceable testing limits
  • A community benefit agreement spelling out local hiring commitments
  • Full public disclosure of the PILOT or tax incentive agreement before any vote
  • A community notification plan for generator testing and emissions events
