💰 Who Really Benefits?

Follow the Money

Data center developers promise jobs and tax revenue. But who actually profits? Who owns the LLCs? Who is the end user? And how do promised community benefits hold up against actual outcomes? This page tracks what's known — and what's being hidden.

⚠ Transparency Problem: Of the five data center projects tracked in Southside Virginia, three involve undisclosed developers or opaque LLC ownership structures. No project has publicly confirmed the end user (the tech company whose servers will occupy the facility). Residents are being asked to approve projects without knowing who they're approving them for.

The LLC Ownership Problem

In Virginia, a limited liability company (LLC) can be formed with minimal public disclosure. Data center developers routinely create project-specific LLCs — names like "Greensville Commerce Park Holdings, LLC" — that reveal nothing about the true beneficial owner.

This means local elected officials may be voting to approve a 400 MW facility, worth billions of dollars to its owners, without knowing who those owners are, whether they have a track record of meeting commitments, or whether a major global technology company is the true beneficiary.

End Users: The Hidden Beneficiaries

The end users of data centers — the companies whose servers run in the facility — are almost never disclosed during the approval process. These end users are almost universally large technology companies: Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Meta, Apple, or similar hyperscalers.

These companies have annual revenues in the hundreds of billions of dollars. They have sophisticated real estate and government affairs teams. They benefit from tax incentives, land prices, and electricity rates negotiated on their behalf by developers — often without their name ever appearing in a public document.

Why This Matters: When a county approves a 10-year tax abatement for a "data center project," they may be giving a tax break to Amazon, Microsoft, or Google — some of the wealthiest companies in human history. Residents have a right to know this before the vote.

The Jobs Promise vs. Reality

Data centers are capital-intensive but not labor-intensive. A 100 MW facility typically employs 20–50 full-time workers once operational. Construction jobs are temporary and often go to workers from outside the region.

⚠ Southside VA Job Promises vs. Scale:
  • Brunswick 120 MW facility: 35 permanent jobs promised
  • South Hill 80 MW facility: 28 permanent jobs promised
  • Greensville 200–400 MW: ~50 permanent jobs rumored

For context: Southside Virginia has been losing manufacturing jobs for decades. Three data centers promising 113 total permanent jobs would be less than the typical rural manufacturing plant. The projects require far more infrastructure investment per job than comparable industrial development.

💼 Who Typically Benefits

Data Center Developer
Acquires land, builds facility, collects lease income from end user. May flip the asset after stabilization.
Technology Company End User
Benefits from tax incentives, low land cost, and cheap electricity — all negotiated on their behalf. Often never disclosed to local government.
Land Sellers
Landowners often receive above-market prices — sometimes 5–20x assessed value. This benefits a small number of individuals.
Real Estate Brokers & Attorneys
Significant transaction and legal fees on large projects. These professionals are often the most visible advocates in local proceedings.
Utility Company
Large industrial customers are major revenue sources — though negotiated rates may benefit the data center while infrastructure costs go to ratepayers.

❌ What Communities Often Don't Get

  • ✗ Significant permanent local employment
  • ✗ Local hiring requirements (usually not binding)
  • ✗ Community benefit agreements
  • ✗ Infrastructure cost protections
  • ✗ Full tax revenue (absorbed by abatements)
  • ✗ Environmental safeguards written into approvals
  • ✗ Right to know the true end user or owner
